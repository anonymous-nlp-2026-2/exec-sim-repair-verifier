\begin{abstract}
LLM-based repair generates candidate patches, selecting the best via a verifier. Three execution-free alternatives to expensive execution-based verification have emerged: per-test binary classification (\cls{}), execution output generation (\cwm{}), and trajectory-level scoring (\traj{}). No prior work compares \cls{} and \cwm{} under matched conditions; the \traj{} comparison is inherently confounded.

We present the first systematic comparison across two code LLM families (Qwen3, DeepSeek-Coder), two complexity levels, and multiple seeds under ${\leq}$8B LoRA fine-tuning. At function level, \cls{} and \cwm{} are statistically equivalent across both families (TOST $p{=}0.007$, 5 seeds): classification is not inherently easier than generation when outputs are short. \traj{} equivalence is inconclusive after multiple comparison correction at current power (${\sim}$0.3), but the gap narrows from 3.11\pp{} (4B) to 1.07\pp{} (14B). At repository level, this equivalence diverges sharply (DeepSeek-Coder): the \cls{}--\cwm{} gap widens to 18--26\pp{} across 2--3 seeds under our ${\leq}$8B LoRA protocol, accompanied by format collapse. Preliminary 14B results (3 seeds, single family) suggest the gap may narrow at larger scale.

Error complementarity and a failure mode taxonomy motivate complexity-aware routing, though simple heuristics capture $<$0.4\pp{} of oracle advantage.
\end{abstract}
